About

I'm a statistician with a Ph.D. from UCLA. My dissertation developed Bayesian methods for social and economic networks, the kind of models that describe how friendships and trade relationships form. As a research data scientist in psychometrics, I built hierarchical Bayesian models that track students' latent skills over time, and released the method as an R package.

At Farmers Insurance I trained gradient boosted risk models that now inform how the company prices and segments auto risk. I'm at my best where all of this comes together: experimentation and causal inference to learn what works, Bayesian modeling to stay honest about uncertainty, and machine learning to act on it.

Projects

Measuring what a math intervention actually taught

First-author paper in Psychometrika (2025) · released as an R package

In a math education study, 849 students took the same 21-question test twice. In between, 423 of them received a new instructional program. Whether the program worked is a harder question than it sounds, because the thing you care about is hidden. Each question draws on underlying skills, such as proportional reasoning, that no single answer measures directly.

I modeled each skill as a hidden state a student can gain or lose between tests, with the gains and losses written as logistic regressions. The intervention enters as a coefficient you can read directly in log-odds. Estimating three coupled layers of latent structure is normally painful, so I used Pólya-gamma augmentation to reduce the whole model to a Gibbs sampler with closed-form updates. It runs fast and it mixes well.

The program clearly raised students’ odds of gaining three of the four skills. For the fourth, the effect was indistinguishable from zero, which is worth knowing too. And the model checks out against reality: response data simulated from the fit tracks the observed data question by question, with AUC near 0.87 at both time points.
